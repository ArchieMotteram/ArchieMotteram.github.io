AI & the Australian Economy (2030 Forecast)

By 2030, artificial intelligence is likely to become one of the most important economic technologies adopted in Australia since the spread of the internet and business software (Brynjolfsson and McAfee 2014).

Its impact will come less from mass job displacement and more from the transformation of cognitive tasks such as drafting, forecasting, coding, classification and decision support (Agrawal, Gans and Goldfarb 2018; Crafts 2021).

This reflects a reduction in the cost of information processing across a highly service-based economy where knowledge work dominates output (OECD 2023).

Australia’s productivity growth has been weak for over a decade, limiting wage growth and living standards (Productivity Commission 2024). AI offers a potential uplift through automation and augmentation of labour.

However, these gains are unlikely to be evenly distributed. High-skill workers and large firms are better positioned to benefit, raising concerns about inequality (Autor 2015; Acemoglu and Restrepo 2018).

Core Argument

AI will increase productivity primarily through augmentation and workflow redesign rather than mass unemployment, but without strong policy intervention it risks deepening inequality across workers and firms (Productivity Commission 2024).

AI as a General-Purpose Technology

AI fits the definition of a general-purpose technology (GPT): a technology that is widely applicable, improves over time, and enables complementary innovations across the economy (Bresnahan and Trajtenberg 1995).

Historical examples include electricity and computing. These technologies did not immediately increase productivity; instead, gains emerged as firms reorganised production systems around them (Crafts 2021).

In Australia, AI is especially relevant because the economy is service-dominated. Many jobs involve information processing, communication and coordination tasks.

Large language models reduce the cost of producing text, analysis and code, while machine learning improves prediction and classification tasks across industries (Agrawal, Gans and Goldfarb 2018).

Key Insight

The productivity impact of AI depends not on adoption alone, but on organisational restructuring and complementary investments such as training and data infrastructure (Productivity Commission 2024).

Economic Transmission Channels

AI affects the economy through four major channels identified in economic literature:

Productivity Channel

AI reduces time spent on routine cognitive tasks such as drafting, summarising and administrative processing, raising output per worker (Crafts 2021).

Labour Augmentation

Most jobs are task bundles. AI substitutes for some tasks while complementing others, reshaping jobs rather than eliminating them (Autor 2015).

Innovation Channel

AI reduces the cost of experimentation and prototyping, increasing innovation capacity across firms (Agrawal, Gans and Goldfarb 2018).

Concentration Channel

Firms with data and capital advantages adopt AI faster, potentially increasing market concentration (Acemoglu and Restrepo 2018).

Industry Impacts

AI exposure depends on task composition and information intensity across sectors.

Professional Services

High exposure due to reliance on analysis, drafting and knowledge work. Junior roles may be most affected as routine tasks are automated (Autor 2015).

Healthcare

AI supports clinicians through diagnostics, triage and administrative tasks, improving efficiency under rising demand pressures (AIHW 2024; Topol 2019).

Education

AI improves teaching efficiency and content delivery but may widen inequality in student outcomes depending on access (OECD 2023).

Government & Finance

AI improves forecasting, compliance and service delivery, increasing state capacity and efficiency (Productivity Commission 2024).

Why Australia Is Especially Exposed

Australia is a highly service-based economy, meaning a large share of employment is in information-intensive sectors where AI has strong applicability (OECD 2023).

At the same time, Australia has experienced prolonged weak productivity growth, which constrains wage growth and fiscal capacity (Productivity Commission 2024).

This creates a dual outcome: high potential productivity gains, but also significant distributional risk.

The central constraint is not invention but diffusion — whether AI spreads across firms, sectors and skill levels (Crafts 2021).

Scenario Analysis (2030)

Baseline: moderate productivity gains with uneven diffusion and slight increase in inequality.
