AI and the Australian Economy

Interactive economics report

Thesis

Artificial intelligence is likely to matter for Australia less as a sudden replacement of all labour and more as a broad reorganisation of cognitive work. The strongest economic effect is likely to come through faster drafting, summarising, coding, customer handling, document review, triage, forecasting and decision support. In a service heavy economy, that matters because so much value creation depends on information processing rather than purely physical production.

The economic case is strongest where AI lowers the time cost of routine or semi routine tasks and lifts the output of skilled workers. The distributional risk appears when those gains are captured mainly by large firms, already productive teams, and workers with stronger digital capability. That creates a key policy question: can AI lift weak productivity growth without deepening existing divides between firms, regions and workers?

This report treats AI as a general purpose technology whose gains depend on diffusion, complementary investment, managerial redesign and workforce capability rather than adoption alone.

What AI has already improved

The clearest improvements so far are in speed, administrative efficiency and task support. Firms report faster processing of documents, shorter turnaround times for internal knowledge work, better drafting, easier search across internal material, and lower time spent on repetitive business tasks. These effects matter because productivity gains usually appear first as time saved inside workflows before they show up more broadly in national statistics.

In Australia, firm behaviour suggests businesses see AI as more than a passing tool. Business R&D spending on AI more than doubled across a short period. That matters because R&D spending is a revealed preference measure. Firms invest where they expect future returns, capability building and competitive advantage.

How to read this site

This website combines observed measurements with scenario modelling. Where official numbers exist, they are shown directly. Where the future is uncertain, the report uses clearly labelled modelled indices so the logic stays transparent. That makes the site more defensible academically and easier to explain in an assessment.

The central idea across all pages is that AI is most likely to reshape tasks, management systems and productivity pathways rather than simply remove whole occupations at once.

Productivity and performance

Australia has a productivity problem that predates the current AI wave. The Productivity Commission reports that multifactor productivity fell by 0.5% in 2024 to 25. That is below the 20 year average annual growth rate of 0.4% and far below the 1.6% average annual increase recorded between 1994 to 95 and 2003 to 04.

This is why AI matters economically. It arrives at a time when Australia needs stronger productivity growth but has struggled to generate it consistently through existing channels. If AI improves business processes, knowledge work and organisational efficiency, it could help lift output per worker. If adoption stays shallow, the gains remain limited.

Observed AI related gains

Survey and experimental evidence shows that AI can already improve workplace outcomes. Deloitte reports that 61% of Australian companies in its survey say AI is delivering improved efficiency. However, only 30% say they are using AI to deeply transform work. That gap matters because incremental gains are easier than full workflow redesign.

The same survey shows 65% of Australian respondents intend to raise AI investment next year, compared with 84% globally. Only 12% of Australian leaders report that generative AI is already transforming their business or industry, compared with 25% globally. That suggests Australia is investing, but scaling more slowly than some global peers.

Industry exposure

AI affects sectors differently depending on task structure. Information intensive, language heavy and rules based work tends to be more exposed. That is why professional services, finance, government administration and parts of healthcare sit near the top of the exposure ranking used in this site.

Sector analysis

Professional services

High exposure because drafting, analysis, document review, coding and client communication are central tasks.

Healthcare

Strong potential through triage, diagnostics support, records handling and admin reduction rather than full clinical replacement.

Education

Useful for lesson support, marking assistance and content generation, but uneven student access may widen gaps.

Finance and government

High exposure because compliance, reporting, service triage and forecasting are structured and data rich.

Australia specific context

Australia is highly exposed to AI opportunity because it is a service dominated economy with a long running productivity challenge. The ABS reports that total business expenditure on research and development reached \$24.4 billion in 2023 to 24, up 18%. Within that, AI spending grew 142% since 2021 to 22, from \$276.3 million to \$668.3 million.

Policy implications

If AI is a diffusion challenge as much as an invention challenge, then policy should focus on capability spread rather than only frontier breakthroughs. That means workforce training, management capability, adoption support for smaller firms, trustworthy deployment standards, and practical public sector use cases.

The strongest policy areas are skills, competition, diffusion and institutional readiness. Productivity gains at frontier firms do not automatically spread to the wider economy.

Priority actions

Lift workforce capability

Focus on practical AI use in real workflows, not just general awareness.

Support small firm diffusion

Help smaller firms move from pilots to integration and redesign.

Protect competition

Monitor concentration in data access, compute, cloud and model dependence.

Use AI in government carefully

Faster approvals and services matter, but review, accountability and trust remain essential.
