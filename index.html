AI & the Australian Economy

By 2030, artificial intelligence will reshape Australia’s economy primarily through task transformation rather than full job replacement. It reduces the cost of cognitive labour across a service-based economy.

Australia’s productivity growth has been weak for years. AI presents a potential uplift through automation, augmentation, and workflow redesign.

However, the benefits will be uneven — concentrated in high-skill workers and large firms unless diffusion is broad.

Key Insight

AI is not a labour replacement shock — it is a productivity reconfiguration shock.

AI as a General-Purpose Technology

AI behaves like electricity or computing — a general-purpose technology that spreads across industries and improves over time.

Its economic impact depends on complementary changes: training, workflow redesign, and organisational adoption.

In service economies like Australia, AI reduces the cost of information-heavy work: law, finance, healthcare, education, and government.

Why It Matters

Historical GPTs only deliver productivity gains when firms reorganise around them — not when they are merely adopted.

Economic Channels

AI affects the economy through multiple reinforcing mechanisms: productivity, augmentation, innovation, and concentration effects.

Industry Impacts

Different sectors face different exposure depending on how information-intensive their work is.

Professional Services

High exposure due to drafting, analysis, and research automation.

Healthcare

Augmentation of clinicians through diagnostics + admin support.

Education

Efficiency gains but unequal student access risks.

Finance & Government

Automation of compliance, forecasting, and service delivery.

2030 Scenario Forecast

Baseline scenario: moderate productivity growth with uneven distribution.
